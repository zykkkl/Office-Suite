# Premium Presentation Standard

Use this guide when generating a presentation that should feel paid, editorial, distinctive, or creative. Templates are references for renderability and spacing only. They must not define the final art direction.

## Core Principle

Every deck needs a custom visual thesis:

- A concrete metaphor tied to the topic.
- A narrative rhythm across slides.
- Slide-specific compositions, not repeated containers.
- Visuals that clarify meaning, not decoration that fills space.

If a slide can be swapped into another deck without edits, it is too generic.

## Template Use

Use templates only for:

- Canvas size and coordinate sanity.
- Known-safe YAML structure.
- Renderer-supported element syntax.
- Baseline spacing and text fit checks.

Do not use templates as:

- A required page layout.
- A default answer to content organization.
- A source of repeated card grids.
- A reason to avoid custom composition.

## Paid-Grade Workflow

Before writing page YAML, define these in `design.md`:

1. **Creative premise**: one sentence, visual and specific.
   - Weak: "clean warm education style"
   - Strong: "a deck that feels like walking through a library after closing, with pages, margins, and reading light as recurring structure"

2. **Slide choreography**: each slide's role in the experience.
   - opener, origin, proof, contrast, data peak, method, pause, closing

3. **Composition map**: one unique composition per slide.
   - Examples: full-bleed crop, editorial split, timeline ribbon, annotation map, oversized number field, quote wall, poster close.

4. **Motif system**: one reusable semantic motif.
   - Book decks: page edge, margin note, bookmark tab, reading lamp cone, shelf spine, citation mark.
   - Tech decks: circuit trace, terminal window, data pulse, node graph.
   - Business decks: decision path, board marker, market map, tension axis.

5. **Anti-template declaration**: identify which slides intentionally avoid cards and what they use instead.

## Layout Variety Requirements

For decks with 6 or more slides:

- At least 5 distinct composition types.
- No more than 2 card-grid slides in the entire deck unless the content is a catalog.
- No repeated title-divider-card structure on adjacent slides.
- At least 2 slides must use an image, diagram, chart, or typographic poster treatment as the primary visual.
- At least 1 slide must be a contrast moment: dark slide, oversized number, full-bleed image, or sparse quote.

## Preferred Composition Types

Use these as flexible patterns, not fixed templates:

- **Editorial opener**: image crop and title block placed asymmetrically.
- **Annotated artifact**: one large object/image with labels around it.
- **Data poster**: one oversized statistic plus small supporting proof.
- **Narrative timeline**: path, ribbon, shelf, road, or margin sequence.
- **Comparison axis**: two poles with tension, tradeoff, or before/after.
- **Quote wall**: one dominant quote, secondary quotes as texture.
- **Process field**: steps embedded in a meaningful scene or diagram.
- **Magazine spread**: large headline, short deck, image or figure.
- **Map/hub**: center concept with satellite details.
- **Closing poster**: sparse, memorable call-to-action.

Cards are allowed only when they are the best information container. They should not be the default.

## Visual Detail Standards

- Use scale contrast: one dominant element per slide should be visibly larger than the rest.
- Use intentional cropping: images should feel art-directed, not simply placed.
- Use whitespace as composition, not leftover space.
- Use labels, tabs, margins, and annotation lines when they fit the concept.
- Prefer native diagrams, editorial typography, and meaningful image crops over decorative shapes.
- Avoid generic blobs, random circles, stock-photo wallpaper, clip art, emoji, and repeated rounded rectangles.

## Content Standards

Paid-grade slides have editorial judgment:

- Rewrite long bullets into sharp claims.
- One sentence on the slide should be quotable.
- Evidence slides must make the conclusion dominant and the evidence secondary.
- Do not invent data or quotes; if sources are unavailable, mark claims as general and avoid numbers.
- Use fewer words with stronger hierarchy instead of more boxes.

## Self-Review

Before rendering, reject and revise if any of these are true:

- Three or more slides share the same skeleton.
- A content slide is only a title plus equal cards.
- The design concept could describe any topic.
- Visual hooks are decorative rather than semantic.
- The deck lacks a memorable contrast moment.
- Images are used as background filler without composition purpose.
